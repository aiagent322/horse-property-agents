How the Escrow Process Works for Horse Property

Escrow is the protected period between an accepted offer and closing day — when due diligence happens, financing is finalized, and ownership legally transfers. Horse property escrows are longer and more complex than residential closings. Here's what to expect.

What Is Escrow?

Escrow is a neutral third-party arrangement where an escrow officer — employed by a title company or escrow company — holds all funds, documents, and instructions from both buyer and seller until all contract conditions are met. Neither party can access funds or transfer title until every condition is satisfied.

The escrow officer doesn't represent buyer or seller. They follow the written instructions in the purchase contract exactly. Their job is to protect both parties by ensuring nothing transfers until everyone has met their obligations.

Note: In some eastern and midwestern states, attorneys handle what escrow companies do in the West. The process and protections are similar — the title company or attorney acts as the neutral closing agent.

The Escrow Timeline: Step by Step

Day 1
Offer Accepted — Escrow Opens
Once buyer and seller sign the purchase agreement, the listing agent sends it to the escrow/title company. Escrow is officially opened. The buyer typically has 1–3 days to deliver an earnest money deposit — usually 1–3% of purchase price — into the escrow account. This deposit is at risk if the buyer backs out without a contractual reason.
Days 1–5
Title Search Begins
The title company searches public records to verify the seller's legal ownership and identify any liens, judgments, encumbrances, or title defects. For horse properties, this includes water rights, easements, mineral rights severances, agricultural deed restrictions, and any unpaid property taxes. Title searches on rural properties can uncover decades of complicated history.
Days 1–10
Inspections and Due Diligence
The buyer's inspection period. For horse properties this typically includes: home inspection, well flow test and water quality, septic inspection, barn and outbuilding structural inspection, pest/termite inspection of all wood structures, arena drainage assessment, and survey review. Horse property inspection periods are typically 10–21 days — longer than residential. Don't let anyone rush this.
Days 5–21
Appraisal
If the buyer is financing the purchase, the lender orders an appraisal. The appraiser visits the property, researches comparable sales, and delivers a report. For horse properties this takes longer than residential — expect 2–4 weeks. If the appraisal comes in below the purchase price, the buyer and seller must negotiate how to handle the gap.
Days 7–30
Loan Processing and Underwriting
The lender processes the buyer's loan application — verifying income, assets, employment, and the property itself. Agricultural and rural properties often require additional underwriting scrutiny. USDA loans, Farm Credit loans, and portfolio lenders familiar with equestrian properties have different timelines than conventional lenders. Expect the full escrow period to be 30–60 days for a financed horse property purchase.
Days 10–30
Repairs, Negotiations, Contingency Removal
After inspections, the buyer may request repairs or credits. This is a negotiation — sellers can agree, counter, or refuse. Once all contingencies (inspection, appraisal, financing, title) are removed in writing, the buyer's earnest money becomes non-refundable. This is a critical milestone. Don't remove contingencies until you're satisfied with due diligence.
Days 28–58
Final Walk-Through
Typically 1–5 days before closing, the buyer does a final walk-through to verify the property is in the agreed condition — agreed repairs completed, no new damage, all included personal property present. For horse properties, confirm: stall fixtures are present if included, arena lighting works, automatic waterers function, irrigation systems are operational.
Closing Day
Funding, Recording, and Keys
The buyer wires closing funds (down payment plus closing costs) to escrow. The lender wires loan funds. The escrow officer disburses funds to the seller, pays off any existing liens, deducts commissions and fees, and sends the deed to the county recorder for recording. Once recorded, ownership has officially transferred. The buyer receives keys and the seller receives proceeds.

What's Unique About Horse Property Escrows

Water Rights Transfer

Water rights don't automatically transfer with the land in many western states — they must be explicitly assigned. Your escrow officer must ensure all water rights, ditch shares, and irrigation district memberships are properly transferred and that any required state agency notifications are filed. A missing water rights transfer discovered after closing is a serious legal problem.

Agricultural Tax Exemptions

If the property carries an agricultural property tax exemption, the buyer must qualify to continue it. If they don't, the county may retroactively assess taxes at the higher residential rate — sometimes for multiple years. Verify exemption eligibility during due diligence, not after closing.

Livestock and Personal Property

Sometimes sellers offer to include horses, equipment, or livestock in the sale. These are personal property — not real property — and must be addressed in a separate Bill of Sale, not the purchase agreement. Mixing real and personal property in a single contract creates legal and lender complications.

Longer Timelines

Horse property escrows routinely run 45–60 days. Rural lenders take longer. Agricultural appraisers take longer. Well and septic inspections add time. Surveys in rural areas take longer. Build this into your timeline and don't commit to a move date before escrow closes.

What Can Delay or Kill an Escrow

  • Low appraisal — resolved through renegotiation, second appraisal, or cash to cover gap
  • Title defects — prior liens, boundary disputes, unprobated estates on the title chain
  • Failed inspection items — structural issues in barn, well below flow requirements, failed septic
  • Lender issues — buyer financing falls through, underwriting requires additional documentation
  • Water rights complications — rights not properly assigned or in dispute
  • Survey discrepancies — actual fence lines don't match legal boundaries
  • Unpermitted structures — barn built without permits may require retroactive permitting or removal
