What Are Disclosures in a Horse Property Sale?

Disclosures are legally required statements a seller must make about known conditions of a property. For horse properties, the disclosure landscape is far more complex than a standard residential sale — and the consequences of missing one can be severe.

What Disclosures Are and Why They Matter

In every U.S. state, sellers are required to disclose material facts — conditions that a reasonable buyer would consider important in deciding whether to purchase and at what price. Failing to disclose a known material fact can expose a seller to lawsuits, rescission of the sale, and damages long after closing.

For horse properties, the disclosure obligation extends well beyond the standard residential checklist. Buyers need information about land, water, zoning, agricultural restrictions, structures, and environmental conditions that have no equivalent in a typical home sale.

State law governs disclosures. Disclosure requirements vary significantly by state. Arizona, California, Texas, and Colorado each have different forms, timelines, and requirements. Always work with a licensed equestrian real estate agent in your state who knows the specific forms required.

Standard Disclosures (Required in Most States)

Seller's Property Disclosure Statement

A comprehensive questionnaire covering known defects and conditions of the property — roof, foundation, plumbing, electrical, HVAC, pests, mold, flooding, and prior repairs. Sellers must answer truthfully to the best of their knowledge. "I don't know" is a legally acceptable answer; lying is not.

Lead-Based Paint Disclosure

Federally required for homes built before 1978. Many older ranch properties, farmhouses, and barns fall into this category. Sellers must disclose any known lead paint hazards and provide buyers with an EPA pamphlet. Buyers have 10 days to conduct a lead inspection.

Natural Hazard Disclosures

In many states, sellers must disclose whether the property is in a flood zone, fire hazard zone, earthquake fault zone, or other designated hazard area. Horse properties in the rural West are frequently in high fire risk zones — critical information for insurance costs and evacuation planning.

Horse Property-Specific Disclosures

Beyond the standard forms, these are the disclosures that make equestrian transactions uniquely complex:

Water Rights and Water Source

One of the most critical disclosures in any rural or equestrian sale. Sellers must disclose the source of water (well, municipal, irrigation district, surface water), the legal status of water rights, any restrictions on use, and known issues with water quantity or quality. In western states, water rights are property — they transfer separately from the land and must be explicitly included in the contract.

Well and Septic Condition

Horse properties on private well and septic must disclose known issues with both systems. Many states require a well flow test and water quality report as part of the transaction. Septic systems must meet current capacity requirements — a 3-bedroom-equivalent septic serving a 6-bedroom home is a material defect.

Zoning and Land Use Restrictions

Sellers must disclose the current zoning designation and any known restrictions on use. Can the buyer keep the number of horses they intend? Are commercial activities (boarding, training, lessons) permitted? Are there conditional use permits in place? Has the property ever been cited for zoning violations?

Easements and Encumbrances

Any rights others have to use or cross your property must be disclosed. Common equestrian property easements include utility easements, access easements for neighboring properties, trail easements, ditch/irrigation easements, and conservation easements that restrict development. Some easements significantly affect the property's value and use.

Agricultural Exemptions and Tax Status

Many horse properties carry agricultural tax exemptions that dramatically reduce property taxes. Sellers must disclose if these exemptions exist and whether the buyer will qualify to continue them. Loss of an ag exemption can increase annual property taxes by thousands of dollars.

Condition of Equestrian Improvements

Known defects in barns, arenas, fencing, and outbuildings must be disclosed. This includes known structural issues in barns, arena drainage problems, fence line encroachments, and permitted vs. unpermitted structures. A barn built without permits may need to be permitted or removed — a significant expense.

Environmental Conditions

Prior use of pesticides, herbicides, or fertilizers on pastures; underground storage tanks (old fuel tanks are common on ranch properties); any known soil contamination; proximity to feedlots or agricultural operations that affect air and water quality. Horse properties are occasionally adjacent to industrial agriculture — this must be disclosed.

HOA and Deed Restrictions

Some horse properties are in planned equestrian communities with HOAs that govern stall count, arena lighting hours, manure management, and noise. Deed restrictions may limit the number of horses, prohibit commercial activity, or restrict fencing types. All known restrictions must be disclosed.

The Difference Between Disclosure and Inspection

Disclosures cover what the seller knows. A professional inspection covers what a qualified inspector discovers. These are complementary — not interchangeable. Buyers should never waive inspection contingencies on horse properties, regardless of what the seller has disclosed.

Critical inspections for horse properties beyond the standard home inspection:

  • Well flow test and water quality analysis
  • Septic inspection and load test
  • Agricultural soil report (if farming or irrigation is planned)
  • Structural inspection of all barns and outbuildings
  • Arena footing and drainage assessment
  • Fence line survey to confirm boundaries match legal description
  • Pest/termite inspection of all wood structures including barn
Buyer protection: Request a copy of all disclosures before submitting an offer. Review them carefully with your agent. Any item marked "unknown" or left blank is a question — get the answer during due diligence, not after closing.

What Happens If a Seller Fails to Disclose?

If a seller knowingly conceals a material defect, buyers may have legal recourse even after closing — including rescission (unwinding the sale), damages for repair costs, and in cases of fraud, punitive damages. Real estate attorneys handle these cases regularly. The statute of limitations varies by state but is typically 3–6 years from discovery of the defect.

This is why experienced equestrian agents insist on thorough disclosures — they protect sellers from post-closing liability as much as they protect buyers from surprises.
